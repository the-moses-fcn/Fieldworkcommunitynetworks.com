Why We Exist

The fiber runs
right past us.
No one built the last mile.

The US 101 corridor through Southern Humboldt passes directly alongside one of the highest-capacity fiber backbones in the state. Most people — including most policymakers — don't know it's there. It has been there for decades.

Commercial carriers have had full access to that backbone for years. They have not built last-mile service into Salmon Creek, Redway, Miranda, Phillipsville, or Garberville. They have not done so because the economics don't work for a profit-driven provider serving a dispersed rural population in rugged terrain. That calculus will never change for a commercial carrier.

It changes for a community institution. Fieldwork Community Networks exists because community capital — not commercial capital — can solve this problem. We are building something a carrier structurally cannot build: locally owned, solar powered, accountable to the people it serves, and financially structured to sustain itself without extracting from its community.

Ask any fire chief in a high fire hazard severity zone whether they want ATAK on the ridges in their district. The answer is not complicated. ATAK — Android Team Awareness Kit — is the situational awareness platform fire agencies across the country use for real-time shared team positioning, incident mapping, and coordination during active operations. It runs on hardware that costs a fraction of what a carrier spends on a single antenna mount. It needs almost no bandwidth over LoRa. It works when everything else is down.

So why isn't it on every ridge in Southern Humboldt? Because the corporations that built those towers have no way to bill for it. There is no revenue model for ATAK on a commercial tower. It doesn't cost much to add — that's almost the insult. The capability exists. The hardware is cheap. The fire departments need it. But it wasn't profitable, so it wasn't built.

Fieldwork builds ATAK into every node by default. Not as a future phase. Not as a premium tier. As the baseline — what the fire department gets the day the node goes up, at no cost to them, because it is embedded in infrastructure that earns its keep through five commercial revenue streams that have nothing to do with the fire department. The community funded the towers. The towers run ATAK. It is now profitable — because the community owns the infrastructure.
