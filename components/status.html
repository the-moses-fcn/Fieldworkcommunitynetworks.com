Grant Submitting

The $3M USDA Community Connect application is in final preparation. Every element a reviewer needs to approve it exists: designed infrastructure, committed partners, registered entity, direct manufacturer relationships, and a community that has waited long enough.

The fire departments receive dedicated broadband, private LTE, and emergency communications at no cost. The same infrastructure generates revenue — tower co-location, ground leases, edge compute participation — keeping money in the local economy rather than sending it to shareholders in cities that have never heard of Salmon Creek.

The Infrastructure Is Already There

The fiber backbone running up 101 didn't require Fieldwork to build. The ridgelines at Pratt Mountain didn't require us to create. The sun over Southern Humboldt doesn't bill by the kilowatt-hour. The raw ingredients for this network have been sitting here for decades. What was missing was the community institution willing to connect them.

USDA's Community Connect program exists precisely for this moment — federal capital directed at community-owned broadband infrastructure in unserved rural areas. The grant application is complete. The partners are committed. The entity is formed and registered.

Fire Season Changed Everything

The 2020 and 2021 wildfire seasons demonstrated at enormous cost what happens when rural emergency communications fail. Delayed mutual aid coordination. Degraded situational awareness. Communication failures between responding agencies during active incidents. These aren't abstract infrastructure problems. They cost operational effectiveness when the cost is highest.

Redway FPD, Salmon Creek VFD, and Garberville FPD are partners not because of technology — but because they have lived the consequences of not having it. This network is emergency infrastructure first. The broadband is the baseline. The resilience is the point.
