Why We Exist

The fiber runs
right past us.
No one built the last mile.

The US 101 corridor through Southern Humboldt passes directly alongside one of the highest-capacity fiber backbones in the state. Most people — including most policymakers — don't know it's there. It has been there for decades.

Commercial carriers have had full access to that backbone for years. They have not built last-mile service into Salmon Creek, Redway, Miranda, Phillipsville, or Garberville. They have not done so because the economics don't work for a profit-driven provider serving a dispersed rural population in rugged terrain. That calculus will never change for a commercial carrier.

It changes for a community institution. Fieldwork Community Networks exists because community capital — not commercial capital — can solve this problem. We are building something a carrier structurally cannot build: locally owned, solar powered, accountable to the people it serves, and financially structured to sustain itself without extracting from its community.

Every Fieldwork deployment runs four independent, non-overlapping systems. Each one serves the community on its own terms. Together they form something more resilient than any single technology can be — a network that keeps working when fire comes, when the grid goes down, and when it matters most.

Community Broadband

Fiber-fed dedicated internet from the Garberville point of interconnect, carried by licensed microwave to ridge nodes at elevation, distributed by point-to-multipoint RF to every household and anchor institution in the watershed. 100 Mbps symmetric. No throttling. No data caps. No commercial carrier terms.

02 ◈

Private LTE Cellular

A CBRS Band 48 private cellular network built from scratch for this territory — not a repeater, not a booster, not a workaround. Real cellular service on standard phones in areas that commercial carriers have never served and will never serve. Fieldwork-issued SIM cards for fire departments, anchor institutions, and community members.

03 △

Emergency Communications

A resilient LoRaWAN sensor and signaling mesh that operates fully independently of the primary network. Environmental monitoring, NOAA-grade weather stations, fire detection telemetry, and an emergency signaling floor that keeps working when primary systems are degraded. Built for the conditions that demand communications most.

04 ☀

Off-Grid Solar Power

Every node — ridge relays, valley distribution points, anchor institution connections — runs entirely on solar with LiFePO4 battery backup providing days of autonomy. No PG&E dependency anywhere in the network. No grid failure risk during the fire conditions that demand communications most.

The Infrastructure Is Already There

The fiber backbone running up 101 didn't require Fieldwork to build. The ridgelines at Pratt Mountain didn't require us to create. The sun over Southern Humboldt doesn't bill by the kilowatt-hour. The raw ingredients for this network have been sitting here, unused, for decades. What was missing was the community institution willing to connect them.

USDA's Community Connect program exists precisely for this moment — federal capital directed at community-owned broadband infrastructure in unserved rural areas. The grant application is submitted. The partners are committed. The entity is formed and registered.

Fire Season Changed Everything

The 2020 and 2021 wildfire seasons demonstrated at enormous cost what happens when rural emergency communications fail. Delayed mutual aid coordination. Degraded situational awareness. Communication failures between responding agencies during active incidents. These aren't abstract infrastructure problems. They cost operational effectiveness when the cost is highest.

Redway Fire Protection District, Salmon Creek Volunteer Fire Department, and Garberville Fire Protection District are all partners in this network not because of technology — but because they have lived the consequences of not having it. This network is emergency infrastructure. The broadband is the baseline. The resilience is the point.

The Replication Model

Salmon Creek is
the proof of concept.
101 is the corridor.

Every rural community along the 101 corridor with sun exposure and proximity to that fiber backbone is a candidate for this model. The proof of concept in Salmon Creek becomes the template. The grant becomes the precedent. The LLC structure becomes the model.

Fieldwork Community Networks is not building one network. We are building the first node in a replicable network of community-owned rural broadband and emergency communications infrastructure across the high fire hazard severity zones of Northern California — a string of community-owned, solar-powered micro-infrastructure sites from the Salmon Creek watershed north through Leggett, Laytonville, Willits, and beyond, each anchored by a fire department, each generating value for the community that hosts it.
