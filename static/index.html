Why We Exist

The fiber runs
right past us.
No one built the last mile.

The US 101 corridor through Southern Humboldt passes directly alongside one of the highest-capacity fiber backbones in the state. Most people — including most policymakers — don't know it's there. It has been there for decades.

Commercial carriers have had full access to that backbone for years. They have not built last-mile service into Salmon Creek, Redway, Miranda, Phillipsville, or Garberville. They have not done so because the economics don't work for a profit-driven provider serving a dispersed rural population in rugged terrain. That calculus will never change for a commercial carrier.

It changes for a community institution. Fieldwork Community Networks exists because community capital — not commercial capital — can solve this problem. We are building something a carrier structurally cannot build: locally owned, solar powered, accountable to the people it serves, and financially structured to sustain itself without extracting from its community.

01

Community Broadband

Dedicated internet via MMBI fiber at the Garberville point of interconnect, carried by licensed microwave to 4 ridge nodes at elevation, distributed by point-to-multipoint RF to 8 valley nodes across the watershed. 100 Mbps symmetric. No throttling. No data caps. No commercial carrier terms.

02

Private LTE Cellular

A CBRS Band 48 private cellular network built from scratch for this territory — not a repeater, not a booster, not a workaround. Real LTE bars on community members' existing phones. Fieldwork-issued SIM cards for fire departments, anchor institutions, and residents in areas commercial carriers will never reach.

03

Emergency Communications

A resilient LoRaWAN sensor and signaling mesh that operates fully independently of the primary network. Environmental monitoring, fire detection telemetry, and an emergency signaling floor that keeps working when primary systems are degraded. Each node runs ATAK — the Android Team Awareness Kit used by fire and emergency response agencies nationwide — over the LoRa layer, so crews maintain shared situational awareness and real-time team positioning even when cellular and broadband are unavailable.

04

Off-Grid Solar Power

Every node runs entirely on solar with LiFePO4 battery backup providing days of autonomy. Dual-fuel inverter generators with fire-rated enclosures provide autonomous backup at every ridge and valley node. No PG&E dependency anywhere in the network. No grid failure risk when it matters most.

Fire Season Changed Everything

The 2020 and 2021 wildfire seasons demonstrated at enormous cost what happens when rural emergency communications fail. Delayed mutual aid coordination. Degraded situational awareness. Communication failures between responding agencies during active incidents. These aren't abstract infrastructure problems. They cost operational effectiveness when the cost is highest.

Briceland VFD, Salmon Creek VFD, and Garberville FPD are partners not because of technology — but because they have lived the consequences of not having it. Briceland VFD's formal support, anchored by a chief who has protected that community for decades, is the kind of endorsement that tells USDA reviewers what no grant writer can: that the people responsible for life safety in this territory believe this network belongs here. This is emergency infrastructure first. The broadband is the baseline. The resilience is the point.

The LoRa mesh nodes run ATAK — Android Team Awareness Kit — giving responding crews shared situational awareness and real-time team positioning over a network that operates completely independent of cellular and broadband. When the fire is burning and the towers are down, crews still know where each other are.

How The Network Sustains Itself

Six capabilities
inside every node.
Five revenue streams out.

A Fieldwork node is not a router in a box. It is a containerized data center — hardened, solar-powered, and designed to carry community service and generate commercial revenue simultaneously. Fire departments receive everything at no cost. Commercial clients pay market rates. The network sustains itself from the difference, and then some.

What lives inside each node

Broadband core

MMBI fiber DIA at the Garberville interconnect, carried by licensed microwave backhaul to four ridge nodes at elevation, distributed point-to-multipoint to eight valley nodes across the watershed. 100 Mbps symmetric to every household. No caps, no throttling, no commercial carrier terms. $59 a month — less than half the cost of the nearest satellite alternative.

Private LTE

Baicells CBRS Band 48 radios on every node run a community-owned LTE core — not a repeater, not a booster, not a workaround. Existing phones work. Existing apps work. Fieldwork-issued SIM cards provide coverage in areas commercial carriers have never served and structurally never will. $35 a month, and the tower revenue stays local.

Commercial compute

Phase 2 deploys enterprise compute infrastructure at the primary Garberville site — financed by venture capital, entirely separate from the USDA grant. Excess capacity is leased to AI and cloud clients at commercial rates around the clock. This revenue stream operates independently of subscriber growth and acts as a financial stabilizer for the entire network.

NOAA weather

Every ridge node hosts a certified weather station: temperature, humidity, barometric pressure, wind speed and direction, UV index — in real time at ridgeline elevation. That data feeds CAL FIRE and the National Weather Service, filling a gap in fire weather monitoring that has never been addressed at this elevation and resolution in Southern Humboldt County.

LoRaWAN mesh + ATAK

RAKwireless gateways on every node anchor a low-power mesh of 135 endpoint devices distributed across the watershed — fire weather sensors, emergency beacons, soil monitors, environmental stations. Each node runs ATAK (Android Team Awareness Kit), delivering cursor-on-target situational awareness to fire crews over LoRa. When broadband and cellular are degraded under active fire conditions, responding crews still share real-time team positions and incident mapping. The mesh operates fully independently of the primary networks. When everything else fails, this layer keeps running.

Telehealth

High-bandwidth symmetric broadband at every anchor institution provides the infrastructure for remote clinical consultations, diagnostic imaging relay, and patient monitoring at the point of care. Health systems pay for the access. Patients receive care that was previously inaccessible without a two-hour drive. The revenue arrives as a monthly institutional fee.

Revenue streams — per node, per month

Each stream is independent. A slow month in broadband doesn't threaten cellular. A below-average compute utilization period doesn't affect tower lease income. Five separate revenue lines from the same infrastructure — no single one of them load-bearing.

The Replication Model

Salmon Creek is
the proof of concept.
101 is the corridor.

Every rural community along the US 101 corridor with ridgeline access, proximity to the MMBI fiber backbone, a fire department willing to anchor, and a population that has been waiting for someone to do this — is a candidate for this model. The proof of concept in Salmon Creek becomes the template. The grant becomes the precedent. The LLC structure becomes the franchise.

Fieldwork Community Networks is not building one network. It is building the first node in a replicable network of community-owned rural broadband, emergency communications, and distributed edge infrastructure across the high fire hazard severity zones of Northern California — a string of solar-powered micro-infrastructure sites from the Salmon Creek watershed northward through the 101 corridor, each anchored by a fire department, each generating value for the community that hosts it.

Phase 2 deploys commercial compute infrastructure at the primary Garberville site — financed separately by venture capital, not grant funds. AI inference, edge processing, and distributed compute generate a revenue stream that further stabilizes the network and funds corridor expansion without returning to federal grants.

Beginning in Year 3, retained earnings from the established Salmon Creek subscriber base self-fund one additional node deployment every eighteen months — no new grant required. There are twenty to thirty viable locations in Humboldt County alone where the same formula holds. Southern Humboldt is the first. It is not the only.
